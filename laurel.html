Laurel

The project

People are eager to achieve their goals, but there is a world of distance between simply setting a goal and actually following through on a plan to reach it. Despite the difficulty, the desire for personal development is sometimes strong enough to reach the finish line, but it is often hard to self-motivate. Especially in the bustle of the modern world, people are looking for resources to support them to make progress towards their goals. This is evidenced by the several billion dollar industry that has emerged for personal development products, which is projected to continue growing over the next decade (Personal development market size, 2022).

However, beyond realizing the market opportunity, our team wanted to develop an application that truly improves the lives of our users. Scientific research has linked the pursuit and attainment of both intrinsic and extrinsic goals to improved overall well-being (Tóth, 2018). This evidence further encouraged our team to pursue the development of what became Laurel, a goal-setting and goal-achieving app.

We aimed for our app to work by assisting the user to set goals with specific time horizons and commitments associated with them, leveraging AI to recommend a goal plan and metrics that will help the user work towards and track their goal. Users can sync their goal plans to their calendars and edit them as needed. There would also be a social aspect to Laurel where users could connect with others that are pursuing similar goals.

Research

Personas & Empathy Maps

We used three well-rounded user personas and associated empathy maps that visualized the users’ behaviors and attitudes to provide a better understanding of what our target audience would look like.

Johanna represents university students. The latter normally have many assignments and due dates, making it hard for them to balance between social life and personal goals.

Henry represents health conscious people. These people may need help managing a disability or reaching wellness goals. Both scenarios require time consuming and detailed activities that are difficult to schedule for and keep track of.

Laura represents parents. They may experience trouble managing their time between their children and careers.

Interviews

Once we determined our target audience, we conducted user interviews to learn about our intended users’ backgrounds and goals. We recruited individuals matching the university student persona and the parent persona however, we had difficulty finding someone to match the health-conscious persona. Instead, we interviewed a working professional for our third interview as an equally likely target user.

There were three high-level aspects that we wanted to explore through the interviews: users’ experience with goal setting and goal achievement, opinions about goal setting and planning aspects of our app, and opinions on the accountability and motivation aspects of our product.

The results of the interviews gave us some insights on which direction to take our app design to achieve highest user satisfaction and also uncovered our incorrect assumptions about users. For instance, we initially planned to incorporate a feature that allowed users to share their goal progress to external apps as we expected people to feel motivated doing so. However, the interviews revealed less interest in this feature than expected.

We used an affinity diagram to categorize the key insights from the interviews. We arrived at five main sections: features and specifications wanted in a goal setting app, current difficulties experienced with goal setting, types of goal users track or want to track, current approaches to goals setting, and concerns or comments on the sharing aspect.
